## Understanding the Challenge

When a child prefers English over their native language, it's essential to understand why. Often, children lean towards the dominant language in their environment due to exposure, interactions with peers, and media consumption. For example, a young student attending an English-dominant school may adapt to peer language norms, reinforcing their preference for English. Studies on bilingual children reveal that language preference is greatly influenced by the language spoken by friends and the media they consume. Recognizing these factors helps parents understand their child's inclination without assuming it stems from a lack of cultural appreciation or familial loyalty. Understanding this challenge sets the stage for emotional balancing and motivation tactics essential for navigating bilingual upbringing effectively.

## Practical Solutions

To address a child's preference for English, parents can implement practical solutions that foster language balance. One effective strategy is integrating both languages naturally into daily routines, ensuring the native language remains a consistent part of the child's environment. For instance, parents can establish family traditions that require using the native language, such as storytelling nights or cooking sessions with recipes in the native language. Research indicates that interactive activities enhance language retention and encourage bilingualism. By incorporating these activities, parents can subtly motivate their child to embrace both languages equally. Providing diverse forms of media in the native language can also create an immersive experience that appeals to a child's interests. This approach not only balances language exposure but also strengthens familial bonds through shared cultural experiences.

For instance, in an English-dominant school, a child may switch to English to fit in with peers and engage in group activities, reinforcing this preference.

Peer influence and media exposure significantly shape language preferences. Studies show that children who consume English media are more likely to favor English in daily interactions. A case study involving a child attending an English-dominant school revealed adaptation to peer language norms as a primary motivation for preferring English.
