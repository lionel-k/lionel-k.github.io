## Understanding the Challenge

Understanding why children do not want to speak their mother tongue is not simple. It involves many factors. There are both psychological and social reasons. Cognitive dissonance plays a big role. Kids sometimes struggle with managing two languages. They may choose the language they hear most at school or outside. For example, a bilingual child in a monolingual school may feel lonely. This can cause stress and even emotional meltdowns. Research shows that children try to copy what their friends do. They want to fit in. In many cases, this means they stop using their native language. It is important to talk honestly about these pressures. Addressing these mental barriers early can help prevent long-term language refusal.

## Practical Solutions

To fix the problem of language refusal, parents need to create a friendly home environment. The key is to expose children to the mother tongue in many ways. Daily conversations, media, and literature are important. For example, using music and stories in the native language can make learning fun. Parents can set routines. They might decide to speak the native language during dinner or bedtime. Research shows that such consistent exposure helps children keep the language. It is also good to practice the language in real life. Encourage kids to join cultural events. This helps them see the native language as practical and useful. These steps can lead to important breakthroughs in language learning.
