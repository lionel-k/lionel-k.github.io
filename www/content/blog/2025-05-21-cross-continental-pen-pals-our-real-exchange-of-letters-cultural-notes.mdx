In the bustling city of Minneapolis, where skyscrapers meet lush green parks, the Ndikumana family navigates a rich blend of cultures. Originally from Burundi, they have settled in the United States, striving to keep their native language, Kirundi, alive in a predominantly English-speaking world. For the Ndikumana children, receiving handwritten letters from friends back in Africa is more than just exchanging words; it serves as a vital link to their heritage. These letters carry tales of African sunsets and family gatherings, reminding them of their roots and connecting them across distances and memories. This emotional connection resonates with African diaspora parents in American cities like Atlanta and San Francisco. They work hard to balance the richness of their past with the promise of the future.

In this unique setting of language learning, African families in the US face unexpected challenges. The findings highlight the importance of integrating cultural nuances into daily life, presenting both obstacles and opportunities for families committed to preserving linguistic heritage in an English-dominant society.

## From Quills to Ballpoints: The Evolution of Correspondence

The journey of pen pals began in the 19th century when handwritten letters became a popular way to communicate. Back then, letters were written with quills and ink. Although this method was cumbersome, it added a personal touch. As the industrial revolution progressed, ballpoint pens were introduced, making writing more accessible. This change made it easier for people to maintain long-distance friendships. Kids engaged in cross-continental pen pal exchanges thrived as more children participated in cultural exchanges through handwritten letters. For example, European countries saw children from different cultures sharing stories and forming lifelong connections. The shift from quills to modern pens symbolizes not only technological advancement but also the growing importance of handwritten correspondence in fostering international bonds.

## The Role of Pen Pals in World Wars

During the world wars, pen pal relationships offered comfort and connection amidst global chaos. Letters were crucial morale boosters, linking soldiers and civilians to the outside world. These exchanges provided emotional support and an escape from war's harsh realities, helping individuals bond across continents. For instance, during World War II, many soldiers corresponded with civilians, sharing personal experiences and cultural notes that created lasting friendships despite the distance and turmoil. Such letters were not just communication tools; they were lifelines bridging geographical and emotional divides, emphasizing the power of words to unite people even during conflict.
