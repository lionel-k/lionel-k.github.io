# Rediscovering Roots: Preparing for a Family Visit Back Home

Visiting family back home is like opening a treasure chest of emotions and cultural discoveries. Consider the Nkurunziza family from Seattle. Their six-year-old son, Amani, was born in the US to Burundian parents. He spoke English at school and Kirundi at home. As their trip to Bujumbura neared, Amani's parents saw he needed to sharpen his Kirundi to communicate well with his grandparents. They used storytelling and language apps to help him. What seemed daunting became a rich dive into his heritage. This is a common journey for many African diaspora families in the US, who strive to keep their native languages alive in an English-speaking world.

Learning African languages in the US has its hurdles and chances. Studies reveal that 70% of African diaspora parents try to teach their native languages at home. Yet, only 30% of their kids become bilingual by their teens. This gap highlights the tricky balance between keeping one's culture and blending into big cities like New York and Los Angeles. While some think African languages can't thrive in America, there are communities proving otherwise. They nurture bilingualism through creative ways suited for urban life.

This guide offers practical tips for US-based families to navigate this landscape. From setting language goals to handling culture shock, we'll explore strategies tested in American settings. You'll find success stories from African communities in cities like Atlanta and Minneapolis, showing techniques that fit seamlessly into everyday life. Get ready to embark on a journey that equips you with tools for a successful home visit while deeply enriching your family's cultural tapestry.

## Understanding the Language Challenge

Getting ready for a family visit involves more than just packing bags; it requires setting language goals to close communication gaps. Knowing your current language skills is crucial. Many people think they speak better than they do, leading to mix-ups during visits. Take a friend who thought she was fluent but struggled with local slang when she went back home. This shows why realistic self-assessment is important. Tools like online proficiency tests or language exchange programs can accurately measure your skills, offering a baseline for setting reachable goals. Recognizing your proficiency helps tailor your learning plan so you focus on what needs improvement rather than what you already know well. With a clear view of your language abilities, you can set precise goals for your visit, ensuring smoother interactions with family and locals.

## Establishing Achievable Language Objectives

Once you've checked your language skills, the next step is setting achievable language objectives. Using the SMART goal framework—Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound—brings clarity and focus to language learning. For instance, if you want to improve conversational fluency, a SMART goal could be: "Practice speaking for 30 minutes daily using language apps or with a partner for three months before the visit." A family member successfully used this approach to enhance their communication skills by setting clear objectives and tracking progress. Setting these goals not only prepares kids for a home visit but also instills discipline and motivation to learn consistently. Adjust goals based on progress to ensure they stay challenging yet attainable. By having well-defined objectives, you're more likely to achieve language proficiency that enriches your visit experience.

### Establishing Achievable Language Objectives

Setting goals using the SMART framework ensures effective preparation for a family visit back home. The SMART criteria help craft goals that are clear and reachable:

- **Specific**: Focus on a particular area.
- **Measurable**: Track your progress.
- **Achievable**: Set realistic targets.
- **Relevant**: Align with the context of your visit.
- **Time-bound**: Set deadlines.

Instead of vaguely aiming to "improve Spanish," a SMART goal would be: "Learn 50 new Spanish words relevant to family conversations by the end of the month." This goal has a clear focus, measurable vocabulary count, achievable study plan, relevant context, and set deadline.

For beginners, SMART goals might include mastering basic greetings or common phrases. Intermediate learners might aim for a ten-minute conversation with relatives in their native language. A family member targeted French conversational fluency through daily practice and weekly checks, significantly enhancing communication skills during their visit. This example highlights how practical planning and emotional readiness, supported by SMART goals, can effectively prepare kids for a home visit, reducing culture shock and enhancing familial interactions.
