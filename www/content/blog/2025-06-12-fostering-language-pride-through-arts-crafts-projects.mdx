In the bustling neighborhoods of Atlanta, Georgia, a Burundian family weaves a tapestry of linguistic heritage and cultural pride through vibrant arts and crafts. When they first arrived in the United States, the Muhoza family faced the fear of their children losing touch with their mother tongue, Kirundi. To combat this, they embarked on a creative journey, transforming their living room into a lively workshop where their children crafted colorful labels for household items in both English and Kirundi. The children's drawings of traditional Burundian scenes adorned the walls, each piece a testament to their dual identity. This simple yet profound practice not only enriched their vocabulary but also instilled a deep sense of pride and belonging, connecting them to their roots while navigating life in America. Their story resonates with countless African diaspora families across U.S. cities who are determined to keep their linguistic heritage alive amidst the challenges of assimilation.
