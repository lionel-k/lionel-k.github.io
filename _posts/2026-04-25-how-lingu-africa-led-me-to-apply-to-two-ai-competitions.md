## Why lingu.africa Exists

When I started lingu.africa, I wasn’t thinking about AI competitions. I was thinking about my niece, who lives in Belgium and speaks French and Dutch fluently but doesn’t know a word of Kirundi, our family’s language.

Kirundi isn’t just a language for me. It’s the sound of my grandmother’s stories, the rhythm of my mother’s lullabies, the secret code my cousins and I used when we didn’t want the adults to understand. It’s identity, memory, family, and cultural transmission wrapped into one.

Yet for many African diaspora families, that transmission is breaking. Parents want their children to speak their heritage language, but they lack modern, structured, and engaging tools. Textbooks feel outdated. Apps built for global languages don’t understand our sounds, our proverbs, our everyday situations.

lingu.africa began as a simple answer to that problem: build a place where African diaspora families can reconnect with their heritage languages, starting with Kirundi. Not as a vocabulary drill, but as a doorway to culture, stories, and identity.

What I didn’t expect was that this doorway would also open two unexpected doors: the China–Africa AI Case Innovation Competition and the Presidential African Youth in AI & Robotics Competition.

## Building Beyond an App

From the beginning, I knew lingu.africa couldn’t be just another language‑learning app. Language is culture. Culture is stories, proverbs, everyday situations, identity transmission. So I built features that reflected that:

- **Story‑based lessons** that teach Kirundi through family narratives, not isolated words.
- **Proverb collections** that carry wisdom and connect generations.
- **Everyday situation dialogues**—how to greet elders, how to talk about food, how to express emotion—that make language usable, not just academic.
- **Voice‑first interaction** because African languages are oral traditions; they’re meant to be heard and spoken.

### The AI Behind lingu.africa

Each of these features is powered by AI—but not in a generic, one‑size‑fits‑all way. I deliberately chose open‑source models that can be fine‑tuned on small, culturally specific datasets. The AI personalizes learning paths, generates culturally relevant stories and proverbs, handles Kirundi‑specific voice interaction, and provides adaptive feedback on pronunciation and cultural nuance.

Each feature was a small step toward a bigger vision: lingu.africa as an AI‑powered cultural and educational project, not just a language app.

That vision is what made the two AI competitions feel like a natural fit.

## Discovering the Competitions

I learned about the China–Africa AI Case Innovation Competition through a newsletter. The Presidential African Youth in AI & Robotics Competition came via a LinkedIn post. Both called for AI projects with real‑world impact in Africa.

My first thought wasn’t “I should apply.” It was “lingu.africa already fits.”

Here’s how I positioned the project for each competition:

### China–Africa AI Case Innovation Competition

**Angle:** AI‑driven cultural preservation and education.

- **Problem:** African languages are disappearing in the diaspora due to lack of engaging, modern tools.
- **Solution:** lingu.africa uses AI to personalize learning, generate culturally relevant content, and adapt to each learner’s heritage background.
- **Impact:** Preserves linguistic diversity, strengthens cultural identity, and empowers diaspora communities.

### Presidential African Youth in AI & Robotics Competition

**Angle:** Youth‑led AI innovation for social good.

- **Problem:** Young Africans need accessible, impactful AI projects that address local challenges.
- **Solution:** lingu.africa is built by a young African founder, uses open‑source AI models, and focuses on a deeply local problem—heritage language loss.
- **Impact:** Demonstrates that African youth can lead in AI, not just consume it, and that AI can be used for cultural preservation, not just commerce.

In both applications, I emphasized the same core idea: lingu.africa sits at the intersection of AI, African languages, education, diaspora identity, cultural preservation, and intergenerational transmission.

It wasn’t a stretch. It was simply naming what the project already was.

## The Competition I Did Not Apply To

Around the same time, I also saw the Africa AI Safety Prize. AI safety is important—critically important—especially for African languages and communities. Bias in language models, misrepresentation of cultural contexts, and unequal access to safe AI are real risks.

I considered applying. But after a few hours of drafting, I stopped.

Why? Because submitting lingu.africa to that competition would have forced me to bend the project too much to fit the theme. I would have had to pivot from “how AI can help preserve African languages” to “how to make AI safe for African languages.”

That’s a valid and needed discussion, but it’s not what lingu.africa is about right now. Not every opportunity is a good opportunity for the project you are building.

**The lesson:** Saying no is strategic. It keeps your focus where it belongs.

## What This Taught Me

### 1. A focused project opens doors.

When you’re clear about what you’re building, you recognize opportunities that genuinely align. You don’t have to chase every shiny call for proposals; the right ones find you.

### 2. Positioning matters.

The same project can be framed for different audiences without changing its core. For China–Africa, I highlighted cultural preservation and education. For African Youth, I highlighted youth‑led innovation and social good. Both were true.

### 3. Saying no is strategic.

Turning down the AI Safety Prize wasn’t a rejection of AI safety. It was a commitment to lingu.africa’s current mission. Focus is a superpower.

### 4. Building in public creates clarity.

Writing about lingu.africa, sharing progress, and talking about the vision helped me articulate exactly where the project fits—and where it doesn’t. That made the application process almost effortless.

### 5. The best opportunities fit naturally.

If you have to contort your project to fit a competition’s theme, it’s probably not the right competition. The right opportunities feel like a natural extension of what you’re already doing.

## The Submission Process

Preparing the applications took about three days of focused work. Each competition asked for different materials:

- **China–Africa AI Case Innovation Competition** required a detailed case study (max 5 pages), a slide deck, and a 3‑minute video pitch. I distilled lingu.africa’s story into a clear narrative: problem, solution, AI‑driven innovation, and impact on cultural preservation. The video was the hardest part—recording a concise, authentic pitch that conveyed both the technical AI aspects and the human story behind the project.

- **Presidential African Youth in AI & Robotics Competition** asked for a project abstract, a technical paper (max 10 pages), and a demo video. Here I emphasized the youth‑led, open‑source, and socially impactful angles. The technical paper let me dive deeper into the AI architecture: which models I used, how I fine‑tuned them on Kirundi data, and how the system adapts to each learner.

Both applications shared the same core, but the framing shifted slightly to match each competition’s focus. The process forced me to articulate lingu.africa’s value proposition crisply—a valuable exercise regardless of the outcome.

## Looking Ahead: Next Steps for lingu.africa

Whether we win a competition or not, the road ahead is clear:

1. **Expand to more languages:** Kirundi is the start. The architecture is designed to support other African languages with similar oral traditions and cultural contexts. Swahili, Kinyarwanda, and Luganda are next on the list.

2. **Deepen AI personalization:** The current adaptive learning paths are a first step. I plan to integrate more granular feedback—detecting subtle pronunciation errors, suggesting culturally appropriate alternative phrases, and generating personalized storylines based on a learner’s family background.

3. **Build a community of heritage‑language learners:** lingu.africa shouldn’t be a solo journey. I’m working on features that let families share progress, celebrate milestones, and connect with other diaspora families learning the same language.

4. **Open‑source the AI components:** To truly serve African languages, the AI models and tools behind lingu.africa need to be accessible to other developers. I’m preparing a release of the fine‑tuned Kirundi models and the training pipeline under an open‑source license.

The competitions are a milestone, not the destination. They’ve already brought clarity, connections, and confidence. Now it’s back to building.

## Closing

I’ve submitted the applications. I don’t know yet whether lingu.africa will be selected for either competition. But the process alone clarified the project’s vision, sharpened its positioning, and reminded me why I’m building it.

Win or lose, the work continues. Kirundi awaits. So do the other African languages we plan to add. And so do the families waiting for a bridge back to their heritage.

lingu.africa is more than an app. It’s a story—one that’s now being told to AI competitions, and one that will keep being told, one lesson, one proverb, one conversation at a time.
